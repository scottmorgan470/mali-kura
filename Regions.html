Mali-kura Regions

Mali-Kura area of 1,241,238 kilometers squared, it is one of the largest African countries. Mali-Kura is bordered by Algeria in the North, Niger in the East, Burkina Faso, Ivory Coast in the South, Guinea in the South-West finally in the West by Senegal and Mauritania.

Mali-Kura is unique because it's common for people to marry outside their ethnic groups and speak languages that differ from those of their ancestors without changing their cultural affiliation. Mali-kura's population is about 24 million with different ethnic groups ( Bambara, Fulani, Senufo, Dogon, Malinke, Bobo, Songhai, Tuareg, and others).

Kaye

The area in which Kayes is situated has an economy based on agriculture. The most important crop is peanuts (groundnuts), and some livestock is raised. Kayes is both the terminus of Sénégal River traffic and an important stop on the Mali Railway (Regie des Chemins de Fer du Mali; in Senegal, Regie des Chemins de Fer du Senegal).

Koulikoro

It is the upper river terminus for large boats on the 1,000-mile (1,600-km) navigable section of the middle Niger River and the last stop on the rail line linking Dakar, Senegal, with this navigable section of the river. The Soninke people in the dry north cultivate millet and sorghum, and the Bambara and Malinke people in the much wetter south grow peanuts, corn (maize), cotton, and mangoes.

Segou

Segou is the headquaters of the Office Niger an extensive irrigation system begun in the year 1932. It is an important agricultural region for Mali and the West African countries (Rice, Millet, Peanuts, Cassava, beans and Cotton, Sugar).

Sikasso

The capital of the Kingdom of Kénédougou in the late 19th century. Today it constitutes a centre for cotton ginning and textile manufacturing.
The Senufo are outstanding musicians, using marimbas, tuned iron gongs, and a variety of drums, horns, and flutes. They are also internationally famous carvers of wood sculpture, mainly masks and figures.

Mopti

located at the confluence of the Niger and Bani rivers. Originally a small fishing village, Mopti has become an important commercial town and the centre of Mali’s fishing and livestock industries. The town is located on three islands and is one of the most densely populated areas in Mali. The major population groups in the locality are the Dogon, Fulani, Bambara, Bozo, Bwa, Songhai, and Tukulor.

Timbuctu

Timbuktu was founded around 1100 CE as a seasonal camp by Tuareg nomads. There are several stories concerning the derivation of the city’s name. According to one tradition, Timbuktu was named after an old woman who was left to oversee the camp while the Tuareg roamed the Sahara. Historically important as a trading post on the trans-Saharan caravan route and as a centre of Islamic culture (c. 1400–1600).

Gao

Gao, founded by fishermen in the 7th century, is one of the oldest trading centres in Western Africa. It is situated on the Niger River at the southern edge of the Sahara, about 200 miles (320 km) east-southeast of Timbuktu. The population consists chiefly of Songhai people. Gao became the capital of the Songhai Empire in the early 11th century.

Kidal

This area was formerly part of Gao Region, it lies 285 km (177 mi) northeast of Gao but was created as a separate region in 1991. It is located in the north of the country. Kidal is mainly Tuareg, it’s a town and commune in the desert region of northern Mali, covering 151,450 square kilometres or 58,480 square miles.
